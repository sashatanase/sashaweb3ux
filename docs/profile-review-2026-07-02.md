## 1. Executive summary

**Verdict: CONTACT — but with reservations a stronger package would eliminate.**

The substance of this profile is well above the senior-UXR bar: real named clients, named methods (diary studies, JTBD, SUS, SEQ, PMF surveys), findings that changed products, and a conference-speaking record (DevCon, EthCC) that most senior researchers simply don't have. The case-study storytelling is genuinely good — "Diary of a Node Operator" reads like the work of someone who knows how to turn research into a narrative.

What holds the profile back is **packaging, proof, and consistency**, not substance:

1. **The resume has no email, phone, or location.** The header is only LinkedIn | Website | Twitter links. An ATS parse produces a candidate with no contact info; a recruiter skimming a printed PDF has no way to reach you. This is the single most damaging defect found.
2. **Zero third-party validation anywhere.** No testimonials, no LinkedIn-style recommendations surfaced, no client logos, no stakeholder quotes. Five of six case studies say "Sole Researcher." Everything is self-reported. Benchmark portfolios (Devin Harold, Mia Eltiste) lead with testimonials.
3. **The resume leaves the last 12 months unaccounted for.** Thesis ends Jul 2025; it is now Jul 2026. Only the Web3UX line ("Apr 2022 – Present") spans the period, and it reads as a side venture rather than what you've been doing full-time. A recruiter's first screening question will be "what have you been doing for the past year?" — the resume should answer it before they ask (contract work, running Web3UX, the 2026 publication and speaking, a deliberate break — whatever is true, dated and framed on your terms).
4. **Small factual errors a research-minded reviewer will catch** (details in §3): "9 years" vs. a 2018 start (8 years); "EthCC[7] Cannes, 2025" (EthCC[7] was Brussels 2024; Cannes 2025 was EthCC[8]); "EthCC[4] Paris, 2022" (EthCC[4] was 2021). For a researcher, citation accuracy is character evidence.
5. **Identity fragmentation:** Sasha (Tanase) Luca / Sasha Tanase-Luca / Sasha Luca / Sasha Tanase across the four channels, with mismatched job titles for the same role. Recruiters cross-reference; every mismatch costs trust.

Fix the contact block, account for the last 12 months, add 4–6 testimonials, and put artifacts + participant quotes into the case studies, and this profile moves from "strong web3 specialist, contact if the role fits" to "contact for any senior UXR role that touches fintech, developer tools, or complex systems."

## 3. Resume review

### What scores well

- **Outcome-driven bullets.** Nearly every Thesis-era bullet is "did X → product changed → metric moved." Most senior resumes never get past task lists. Keep this pattern.
- **A memorable thesis statement.** "Explorative research helps build the right thing, iterative research helps build things right" is a genuinely good positioning line — it shows you think in frameworks.
- **Specialization is legible in one line:** Discovery, Research Ops & Developer Experience. DevEx research is a scarce, in-demand specialty; owning it is smart.
- **The nested Thesis → Mezo / Threshold / Keep structure** handles the venture-studio problem well; a recruiter can see continuity and progression (Lead → Manager).
- **Talks section.** Six talks across DevCon and EthCC is a differentiator almost no senior UXR candidate has. Keep it exactly where it is.

### What must change

1. **Add a contact block (critical).** Header currently reads only `LinkedIn | Website | Twitter`. Add: email (sasha@web3ux.org, matching the site), location + timezone ("Bucharest, RO (EET) · Remote-friendly"), and a GitHub link (it's part of your DevEx story). Without an email the resume is a dead end in every ATS and every forwarded-PDF scenario.
2. **Account for Jul 2025 → present.** The last dated employment ends Jul 2025 — a year ago. Whatever the true story is, the resume should tell it explicitly rather than leave it to the recruiter's imagination: if you've done contract/consulting work, list it as a dated entry with an outcome bullet; if Web3UX has been your focus, re-date and re-frame that entry as your current operating role ("Founder & Researcher, 2022–present; full-time since 2025") with concrete activity; if it was a deliberate break, one honest line ("2025–2026: sabbatical; published X, spoke at Y") beats silence. An unexplained year is the most common silent-rejection trigger at senior level, and yours is fixable with truthful framing alone.
3. **Fix the arithmetic: "9 years."** Your earliest listed role starts Jul 2018 — that's 8 years as of mid-2026, and the site's own About says "2018 — Present." Either write "8+ years" or explicitly count pre-2018 design work by listing it (one line: "Earlier: architecture & design practice, 2011–2018" also explains the post-M.Arch years). A reviewer who catches the resume overstating tenure by a year discounts every other number on the page.
4. **Fix the talk citations.** EthCC[7] was **Brussels 2024**; the Cannes 2025 edition was **EthCC[8]**. EthCC[4] was **2021**, not 2022 (Paris 2022 was EthCC[5]). Verify each entry against the conference archive. Small errors, outsized signal for a researcher.
5. **De-duplicate the opening.** "9 years guided by one principle… 9 years leading UX research…" — same number twice in two sentences. Keep the principle, cut the second "9 years."
6. **Give the metrics provenance (or round them).** 20%, 22%, 18%, 16%, 30%, 15%, 33%, 87%… A senior screen *will* probe two of these. For the 2–3 numbers you'd stake an interview on, add the basis ("per product analytics, 8 weeks post-launch," "n=12"). Consider softening the rest to "~20%" or moving them out of every single bullet — when *every* bullet ends in a percentage, each one is worth less.
7. **Rebalance Selfkey and Consensys.** These two entries are pure task lists ("Conducted… Organized… Worked on…") — the contrast with the Thesis section makes them read like filler. Either compress each to 2 lines or add one outcome bullet each. As Head of Design at ConsenSys/Alethio you presumably led people — headcount and scope belong here (it's your only formal "Head of" title; use it).
8. **Sharpen the Web3UX entry.** "Co-founded, now the single owner" reads as an ownership status update, not an achievement. Add scale ("panel of N web3-native testers, used in M studies for K clients") or cut to one line. Also clarify it ran alongside Thesis, so it doesn't read as a full-time role that double-counts years.

### What to remove or demote (attention-shifters)

- "Created a custom research methodology **I am very proud of**" — the pride editorializes; the method (diary + interview hybrid for node operators) speaks for itself.
- The parenthetical "(before AAVE)" on Coverage Pools needs a rewrite — as punctuated, its meaning is unclear to anyone who doesn't already know the history ("the first feature of its kind, predating AAVE's equivalent" if that's the claim — and be sure it survives fact-checking).
- The two-line Keep/Coverage-Pools bullets partially repeat each other (two bullets both introduce Coverage Pools); merge.

## 4. Website review (sasha.web3ux.org)

*The live site returned HTTP 200 in ~1.4s at review time; a transient 503 was observed once. The daily recruiter-log shows the site has been stable since the 2026-06-22 asset fix. Keep the uptime monitoring habit.*

### What scores well

- **Distinctive, confident visual identity** — the editorial grid, mono labels, and §-numbered sections read "senior" without a single stock illustration. It's more memorable than most benchmark sites.
- **Availability signal** ("Available · 2026") in the header, a mailto CTA in the hero, and a full contact block — friction to reach you is near zero.
- **Six real case studies with meta tables** (client, method, sample, role) and **downloadable full research reports.** Publishing actual reports is the single rarest thing a UXR portfolio can do; it's your version of Cyd Harrell's "radical transparency" and you should keep and advertise it ("Full, unredacted reports available for every study").
- **Case-study narrative structure** — Challenge → Approach ("Capturing Friction in the Wild") → Plot Twists (Assumption vs. Reality) → Impact — is exactly what hiring managers say they want (process, not just deliverables). The tBTC diary study's three findings (hardware-downgrade slashing risk, false-success ASCII banner, three-address overload) are portfolio gold.
- **Recent writing** — a May 2026 article keeps the site alive; recruiters check dates.

### What must change

1. **Add social proof (highest-impact single change).** There is not one third-party voice on the entire site. Benchmarks: Devin Harold has three testimonials plus client logos; Mia Eltiste has four. You have 7 years of colleagues at ConsenSys, Thesis, Mezo, Threshold. Get 4–6 short quotes (a PM, an engineer, a founder, a fellow researcher), put 2–3 on the home page and one at the end of each case study. Ask for the same text as a LinkedIn recommendation so both channels benefit.
2. **Put artifacts in the case studies.** Every case study is wall-to-wall text. The `CaseStudyQuote` component exists in the codebase but is **used zero times** — no participant quote appears anywhere. Add per study: 1–2 verbatim participant quotes, and 2–3 visuals (sanitized diary excerpt/GitHub Gist screenshot, the journey map, the affinity map, a before/after UI shot, the SUS distribution). Hiring managers consistently rank artifacts and evidence directly behind process in what they screen for.
3. **Show the humans you worked with.** Five of six studies say "Sole Researcher," and the layout has no field for team or stakeholders. NN/g's guidance is explicit: state your role *and credit collaborators*. Add a "Team / Stakeholders" row to the meta table ("Sole researcher, embedded with 2 PMs + 4 engineers; findings delivered to exec team") and a sentence per study on how findings landed (the readout, the roadmap meeting, the fight you lost).
4. **Add your strongest strategic story as case study 07.** The resume's best material — the Mezo PMF program (segmentation that refocused the product, the insight repository serving 15+ stakeholders, the 20% qualified-traffic uplift) — is **not on the website**. The six current studies skew evaluative/usability. Senior and staff screens look for research that shaped strategy. Write "Finding product-market fit for Mezo" (or "Building a research function from zero") as the seventh, and put it first.
5. **Sharpen the hero to one role.** "Currently open to UXR **and PM** roles, full-time or contract" hedges. Every benchmark portfolio makes one claim ("I lead effective UX teams"). Lead with Senior UX Researcher; mention product-management range in About ("my background sits between research and product" already does this well).
6. **Add a short "How I work" block.** Mia Eltiste's three-principles section is what makes her read as strategic rather than executional. You already have the material: your explorative/iterative principle, the DX framework from your EthCC talk, your AI-workflows framework. Three principles, one paragraph each, on the home page.
7. **Add a retrospective line to each case study.** Theo Johnson's differentiator is a "what I'd do differently" section — it signals seniority and self-awareness cheaply. One honest paragraph per study ("With more than 5 participants I'd have…", "I under-weighted X") also pre-answers the constraints question (n=5–12 everywhere).
8. **Acknowledge the domain, plan the bridge.** All six studies are crypto-infra. The framing fix is cheap: one sentence in About positioning web3 as *the hardest usability environment there is* ("if users can lose funds irreversibly, research rigor isn't optional") — this converts "niche specialist" into "specialist in high-stakes, trust-critical systems," which generalizes to fintech, security, dev tools, health. The content fix is adding credible non-crypto evidence as it materializes (see §9, item 12). Longer-term: the `sasha.web3ux.org` domain itself brands you web3-only; if you broaden, a neutral domain (sashaluca.com) with a redirect is worth it.

### Minor

- Home "Selected work" labels Thesis as "UX Research Manager / Ops" while the resume says "UX Research & Product Design Lead" — same role, two titles (see §8).
- Footer "AI-assisted, human-obsessed, coffee-fueled" — fine, keep; it's the only personality note on the page and it's honest about the AI assistance.

## 6. GitHub review

For most UXR candidates GitHub is irrelevant. For someone selling **Developer Experience research**, it's a stage — and yours is half-set.

**What scores well:** `mezo-insights` ("a centralized repo for product research and marketing insights, structured, searchable, designed to grow with each study") is a *public research repository* — concrete proof of your research-ops claim that almost no other UXR candidate can show. The `threshold-network` affiliation and the KeyFi user-research report support the story. Running a diary study **through GitHub Gists** (case study 01) is exactly the DevEx-native credibility this channel should broadcast.

**What must change:**

1. **The bio is a blank.** "Alakazam! 🍑" tells a recruiter arriving from your site nothing. Change to: "Senior UX Researcher — discovery, research ops & DevEx in web3/fintech · sasha.web3ux.org".
2. **Add a profile README** (repo `sashatanase/sashatanase`): three lines on who you are, links to the site/talks, and a "featured research" list pointing at `mezo-insights`, the KeyFi report, and `UX-User-Research`.
3. **Curate the repo list.** `GitTutorial` (personal git learning notes) and `Information-Unification` ("tracker for syncing resume, LinkedIn, GitHub, and website") actively undercut the professional surface — the latter exposes the machinery behind your personal brand, which reads oddly if a recruiter finds it. Make both private, along with anything else that's scaffolding. Pin the three research repos.
4. **Publish your frameworks.** The DX-in-web3 framework (EthCC talk) and your AI-research-workflow framework as markdown repos — plus a sanitized discussion-guide/diary-study template pack — would make you the rare researcher whose GitHub is itself a portfolio. Low effort, high differentiation.
